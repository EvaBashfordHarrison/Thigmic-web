## Description 
Thigmic is a language system to prove non-human intelligence in plant species. Designed to be viewed with the Thigmic Reader; an ECG module picking up plant signals. 

### Web app 
The web app is designed for users to interact with the language system, with a single glyph viewer, and a 'typing' mode. 

## Abstract 
Thigmic is a generative written language which translates plant electrical activity into a visual glyph system, as a proposed alternate framework for interpreting non-human intelligence. This research looks at the cross-section between non-human communication and the hierarchy of intelligence that insists humans are on top. 

In Western culture, there is often an anthropocentric view of intelligence, placing humans at the top of a hierarchy and plants at the bottom. Other cultures see this flipped, with humans reliant on plants and animals, therefore sitting below them (Schlanger, 2024). The term “umwelt” means “an organism’s understanding of the world based on only what it knows”. (Bridle, 2018), and it is argued that a language system is limited to ones Umwelt. Non-human communication systems, such as honeybees and their “waggle dance” demonstrate that meaning can be constructed through embodied, species-specific mechanisms rather than symbolic language alone. 

Thigmic extends this perspective to plants by treating electrical signalling as a form of expression. Using sensors to capture Local Electrical Potential (LEP), the system records sub-threshold voltage fluctuations (Lou, 1996; Leng, 1998; Wang et al., 2001; Huang &Wang, 2002; Hu, 2003; Pyatygin et al., 2006) in response to environmental stimuli including light, touch, and changes in surrounding conditions, therefore a measurable reading is produced when plants have a cognitive surge. These signals are streamed into a custom-built web application, where they are processed and translated into a generative visual language. 

In designing this language, speculative influences like Jenna Sutela and her language Nimiia Cetii, as well as Heptapod B from the film Arrival (Arrival, 2016), both ignore anthropocentric language rules and reflect the experiential interpretation of life as that species. Designing a written language for plants needed to feel foreign to humans, but reflective of the plant’s Umwelt. In plants, each new leaf grows at 137.5° to the previous, ensuring the whole plant gets enough light (link), a visual gift hard-coded into its identity. For its movement, at the end of each plant root is a tiny sensor analysing its space and surroundings, meaning the root wiggles and worms its way through soil, dodging obstacles, to keep growing wherever it can. (the light eaters). The glyphs aren’t drawn; they are grown, and through the code, they follow the same growing pattern as real roots, to not grow on top of other nodes. Thigma, being Latin for touch, the name Thigmic references the movement of the plant, its ability to feel its surroundings, and its ability to decide where to go next, proving its self-awareness. The language is a node growth structure. Each glyph represents a voltage reading of electrical intensity in that moment. If the reading is high then there are more branches on the glyph, but if it’s lower, there are fewer. 

The outcome is an experienced piece, a real-time generative system, combining the hardware and the software to provide audiences with a sense that plants are thinking, feeling, working. The signal is picked up by the plant, and on the screen, the written language is produced every 600ms, and between -330v and +330v. If the voltage is between -30 and +30, it is assumed it’s noise and is skipped, in order to only print spikes. The piece can play forever, listening to plants’ thoughts and printing a foreign language.
